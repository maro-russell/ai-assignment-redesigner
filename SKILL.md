# AI Assignment Redesigner

This skill helps faculty transform existing assignments into ones that:
1. Target a specific **AAC&U VALUE Rubric** learning outcome (default: Creative Thinking)
2. Make **AI use transparent and pedagogically intentional**
3. Follow the **TILT Framework** (Purpose / Task / Criteria)
4. Name which **UT Austin Responsible Adoption of AI** principles are being addressed
5. Identify which **UNESCO AI Competency** dimensions students are developing

---

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Gather Inputs

Before redesigning, collect what you need. Ask the faculty member for:
- **The existing assignment** (paste text, upload file, or describe it)
- **Which AAC&U VALUE Rubric** to target (default: Creative Thinking — see `references/aacu-creative-thinking-rubric.md`)
- **Which rubric level(s)** to aim for (Benchmark / Milestone 2 / Milestone 3 / Capstone), or ask what level students are at
- **AI use stance**: Choose one or ask the faculty member:
  - *Exploratory*: Students experiment with AI and reflect on it
  - *Collaborative*: AI as a tool/partner at specific stages
  - *Critical*: Students analyze AI outputs and compare to their own work
  - *Restricted with disclosure*: AI limited but students must disclose any use
- **Course context**: Level (intro / upper-div / grad), discipline, class size (optional but helpful)
- **Output format**: Ask whether they want a **compact** or **full** version:
  - *Compact*: A concise, paste-ready assignment prompt (1–2 pages). Streamlined Purpose, bulleted Task steps, brief Criteria. Best for faculty who want to adapt further themselves, or for posting directly to an LMS.
  - *Full*: A complete, student-facing assignment document with elaborated Purpose rationale, fully sequenced Task steps with guidance notes, a rubric alignment table, and the AI Use Log. Best for faculty who want to hand it directly to students with minimal editing.

The UT Austin principles and UNESCO AI competency dimensions are **always included** in the Purpose section regardless of format — they are part of the pedagogical transparency the assignment offers to students and faculty alike. You do not need to ask for these; derive them from the assignment context using the reference files.

If the faculty member provides the assignment but not the other details, make reasonable defaults explicit and note them. Default to **full** if no preference is given, but mention that a compact version is available.

---

### Step 2 — Analyze the Existing Assignment

Before redesigning, briefly note:
- What the assignment currently asks students to do (task)
- What learning it seems to target (implicit purpose)
- Where AI use is currently addressed (or not)
- Which rubric dimensions are already present vs. absent

Keep this analysis to 3–5 bullet points. Share it with the faculty member so they can confirm or correct your read before you redesign.

---

### Step 3 — Redesign Using TILT + VALUE Rubric + AI Transparency

Use plain language suitable for students. Produce the redesigned assignment in the format the faculty member selected:

---

#### FORMAT A: Compact Version
Use when the faculty member wants a concise, paste-ready prompt they can adapt or drop into an LMS. Aim for 400–600 words total. Hit every TILT element but stay lean — no extended rationale, no inline guidance notes.

```
# [Assignment Title]
**Course:** [Course] | **Due:** [Date]

## Purpose
[2–3 sentences: what skills, what knowledge, why it matters beyond the course.
One sentence on AI's role in the assignment.]

**Responsible AI principles this assignment addresses** *(UT Austin framework)*:
[List 2–4 of the 8 principles — Literacy, Ethics, Intention, Balance, Agency,
Relationships, Academic Integrity, Stewardship — that are most active in this
assignment. One phrase each explaining how.]

**AI literacy dimensions students are developing** *(UNESCO AI Competency Framework)*:
[Name 1–3 UNESCO competency blocks being practiced — e.g., Human Agency (Understand),
Embodied Ethics (Apply), AI Foundations (Understand). One phrase each on how.]

## Task
1. [Step — active verb, specific action, AI permissions noted inline]
2. [Step]
3. [Step]
4. Submit your AI Use Log and a [X]-word reflection addressing: [1–2 focused questions]

**Avoid:** [2 common mistakes, one line each]

## Criteria
Assessed using the AAC&U Creative Thinking rubric at the [Level] level.
Strong work will: [3 bullet points — concrete, observable, student-actionable]
Your AI Use Log and reflection are part of the grade.

---
**AI Use Log** *(attach to submission)*
| Step | Tool | Prompt | Output | What I Did With It |
|------|------|--------|--------|--------------------|
```

---

#### FORMAT B: Full Version
Use when the faculty member wants a complete student-facing document ready to hand out with minimal editing. No length target — be as thorough as the assignment requires. Follow this structure:

```
# [Assignment Title]
**Course:** [Course name/number if provided]
**Due:** [Leave blank or use placeholder]

---

## PURPOSE

### Why this assignment matters
[2–3 sentences explaining real-world relevance and connection to the course.
Avoid academic jargon. Answer: "Why should students care?"]

### Skills you will practice
[List 2–4 skills drawn from the targeted VALUE rubric dimensions.
Name them explicitly in student-friendly language.]

### Knowledge you will build
[1–2 sentences on the disciplinary content knowledge involved.]

### How AI fits in
[Explain the role AI will play — or won't play — and WHY this choice was made.
Be explicit about what AI can and cannot do well for this particular task.
Connect AI use to a specific learning goal.]

### Responsible AI principles this assignment addresses
*(UT Austin Framework for Responsible Adoption of AI in Teaching and Learning)*

[Select and name 2–4 of the 8 principles most active in this assignment:
Literacy / Ethics / Intention / Balance / Agency / Relationships /
Academic Integrity / Stewardship.

For each, write 1–2 sentences explaining concretely how this assignment
engages that principle — not just that it does. Use student-facing language.

Example: "**Literacy** — You'll build fluency in how AI image tools interpret
prompts, and begin to recognize what kinds of instructions produce useful
results versus generic ones."

See references/ut-austin-and-unesco-frameworks.md for all 8 principles.]

### AI literacy you are developing
*(UNESCO AI Competency Framework for Students)*

[Name 1–3 UNESCO competency blocks this assignment activates.
For each, state: the aspect (Human-centred mindset / Ethics of AI /
AI Techniques and Applications / AI System Design), the competency block
name, and the progression level (Understand / Apply / Create).

Write 1–2 student-facing sentences on what they are actually doing that
develops this competency.

Example: "**Human Agency (Understand)** — By tracking where AI's suggestions
didn't match your vision and documenting how you overrode them, you're
practicing the core skill of keeping humans in control of creative decisions."

See references/ut-austin-and-unesco-frameworks.md for the full competency matrix.]

---

## TASK

### What you will do
[Numbered steps. Use active verbs. Be specific about sequence, format, length,
and any checkpoints. Include where and how AI may/must/may not be used at each step.]

### What to avoid
[2–3 common mistakes or misunderstandings to sidestep.]

---

## CRITERIA FOR SUCCESS

### How your work will be evaluated
[Reference the specific VALUE rubric being used by name. Translate rubric language
into plain terms students can act on. Map 3–5 rubric dimensions to concrete
observable behaviors in the student's work product.]

### Rubric alignment
[Include a simple table: Dimension | What "strong work" looks like | What "developing
work" looks like. Draw directly from the VALUE rubric language but translate it.]

### What excellent looks like vs. adequate
[A short paragraph contrasting high and low performance in plain language.]

---

## AI USE LOG
*(Submit this with your final product)*

| Step | AI Tool Used | Prompt I Gave | Output I Received (summary) | How I Used / Changed It |
|------|-------------|---------------|----------------------------|------------------------|
|      |             |               |                            |                        |

**Reflection ([word count]):** [1–2 focused reflection questions drawn from the
AI stance and rubric dimensions being assessed.]
```

---

### Step 4 — Offer Optional Add-ons

After delivering the redesigned assignment, briefly offer:

1. **A faculty-facing rationale** (1 paragraph): Explains the pedagogical choices made — useful for syllabi, IRB documentation, or department sharing.
2. **A student-facing AI policy statement** (2–4 sentences): A ready-to-paste course policy that explains the AI use stance for this assignment specifically.
3. **A peer review protocol**: A structured peer feedback form aligned to the VALUE rubric dimensions used.
4. **A reflection prompt**: A short post-assignment prompt asking students to assess their own creative thinking process using rubric language.

Ask which (if any) the faculty member wants.

## Key Principles to Maintain Throughout

### TILT Framework
Every redesigned assignment must explicitly address all three:
- **Purpose**: Why does this assignment exist? What skills + knowledge will it build?
- **Task**: Exactly what actions should students take, in what order?
- **Criteria**: What does success look like, in concrete, observable terms?

See `references/tilt-framework.md` for detailed guidance.

### AAC&U VALUE Rubric Alignment
- Always name the specific rubric and dimension(s) being targeted
- Translate rubric language into student-accessible terms
- Map at least 3 rubric dimensions to the assignment
- Calibrate expectations to the rubric level appropriate for the course

See `references/aacu-creative-thinking-rubric.md` for the full Creative Thinking rubric.

### AI Transparency Principles
- AI use must serve a **named learning goal** — never use AI "just because"
- Students must be able to articulate what AI did vs. what they did
- The AI Use Log is the accountability mechanism — always include it when AI is permitted
- Make explicit what AI *cannot* do for this assignment (this is often as important as what it can do)
- AI stances exist on a spectrum; help faculty choose one intentionally rather than defaulting to blanket permission or blanket prohibition

### UT Austin Responsible Adoption Principles
- Always select the 2–4 principles **most actively engaged** by the assignment — not all 8
- Name them explicitly and explain concretely how the assignment engages each one
- These serve double duty: they give faculty a shared institutional vocabulary AND give students insight into *why* this AI use is intentional
- If the assignment restricts AI, the principles of **Intention** and **Agency** are almost always in play

See `references/ut-austin-and-unesco-frameworks.md` for all 8 principles with descriptions.

### UNESCO AI Competency Alignment
- Select 1–3 competency blocks — the ones the assignment genuinely activates, not a comprehensive list
- Always name: Aspect → Competency Block → Progression Level
- Translate the competency language into what the student is actually *doing* in the assignment
- For introductory courses, Level 1 (Understand) and Level 2 (Apply) blocks are most appropriate
- The Human-centred mindset and Ethics of AI aspects are almost always present when AI use is transparent

See `references/ut-austin-and-unesco-frameworks.md` for the full competency matrix.
